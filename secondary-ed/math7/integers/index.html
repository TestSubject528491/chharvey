Unit 1: Operations on Integers

Updated Jan 31, 2014.

This unit focuses on performing, and identifying properties of, operations on whole numbers and integers. Students will simplify expressions involving the four basic operations, and will identify the properties that define a mathematical field and apply those properties in the context of the field of Real Numbers.

Learning Objectives

Integer Operation Models

  • Use manipulatives and pictorial representations to model addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division of integers (quotients of integer division problems will be integers as well). Manipulative representations will include two-colored counters, blocks or tiles, and number lines.
  • Identify expressions with inverse operations that are equal, even though they are modeled differently.
    • a-b = a+-b
    • a\div{b} = a\cdot\frac{1}{b}

Integer Arithmetic No Calculator

  • Compute the sum, difference, product, and quotient of two integers (quotients of integer division problems will be integers as well).
  • Determine whether a sum, difference, product, or quotient of two integers will be positive or negative, given those integers.
  • Determine whether a sum or difference of two integers will be positive or negative, given those integers’ locations, relative to each other and relative to 0, on a number line.
  • Determine whether a product or quotient of two integers will be positive or negative, given those integers’ locations, relative to each other and relative to 0, on a number line.
  • Solve word problems involving adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing integers.

Order of Operations

  • A numeric expression is an expression with at least one number, at least zero operations, and exactly zero variables.
  • A variable expression is an expression with at least zero numbers, at least zero operations, and at least one variable.
  • Identify the terms in a given numeric expression or a variable expression, and classify such an expression as a monomial, binomial, trinomial, or polynomial.
  • Compute the value of a numeric expression (containing at least two, but no more than four operations—exponents considered) involving:
    • the four basic operations on integers, and
    • the same with parenthesis and exponents included (the exponent may be applied to a monomial or polynomial).
  • Decide whether a set of grouping symbols in an expression is meaningful: whether the value of the expression would change if the grouping symbols were removed.
  • Evaluate a variable expression with at most three variables and at least one but no more than four operations, given the replacement values of those variables.
    • Given an expression and the variables’ replacement values, find the value of the expression.
    • Given the variables’ replacement values and a target value, choose an expression, that when evaluated, is equal to the target value.
    • Given an expression and a target value, choose a set of variables’ replacement vales that would make the expression equal to the target value. This is identical to choosing values that satisfy an equation.
  • Create a numeric expression equal to a target value, using any of the four basic operations, exponents, and grouping symbols. A set of required numbers may or may not be given.

Field Properties

  • Identify the property illustrated by a given equation. The following properties will be explored, algebraically as well as pictorially:
    • commutative property of addition (CA)
    • commutative property of multiplication (CM)
    • associative property of addition (AA)
    • associative property of multiplication (AM)
    • identity property of addition (IA)
    • identity property of multiplication (IM)
    • inverse property of addition (NA)
    • inverse property of multiplication (NM)
    • zero property of multiplication (ZM)
    • distributive property of multiplication over addition (DM)
    • distributive property of exponents over multiplication (DE)
