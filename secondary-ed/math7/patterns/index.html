Unit 4: Patterns & Relations

Author
Chris Harvey
Updated
Jan 31, 2014
Mar 19, 2014 @ 13:09

This unit explores patterns of integers and rational numbers and relationships between two variables. Students will analyze and generalize sequences of integers and rational numbers. They will also explore different forms of relations using a variety of representations, and will identify and derive rules for functions.

Learning Objectives

Sequences

  • Investigate sequences of rational numbers. Sequence rules will be recursive, that is, the next term in the sequence will be determined by its predecessor. Recursive sequences will be represented by variable expressions of the following forms, where \(p\) represents the previous term and \(n\) represents the index:
    • arithmetic: \(p+d\)
    • geometric: \(p\cdot{r}\)
    • quadratic: \(p+\left(a_n\right)\), where \(a_n\) is an arithmetic sequence
    • factorial: \(p\cdot{n}\)
  • Given an arithmetic sequence, find the common difference (\(d\)).
  • Given a geometric sequence, find the common ratio (\(r\)).
  • Given an arithmetic or geometric sequence, extend the sequence or find a specified term.
  • Create a sequence given a variable expression that describes the pattern.

Relations

  • Graph individual points in the Cartesian plane.
    • given an ordered pair, plot the point in the plane
    • given a point in the plane, determine its x- and y-coordinates
    • identify Quadrants in the Cartesian plane
  • Represent and convert finite relations among a set of ordered pairs, a table, a graph, and an oval diagram. Finite relations may or may not be linear.
  • Represent and convert infinite, linear relations among a representative table (comprising of sample values), a graph, or an equation (of the form \(y=\frac{p}{q}x+b\), or \(x=b\), where \(p\), \(q\), and \(b\) are integers).
    1. Match a relation’s graph and a representative table.
    2. Construct a representative table of a relation given as an equation.
    3. Graph a relation given as an equation. Students will graph relations on a point-by-point basis, using sample points. Students will calculate the outputs of sample points given the inputs, and will also choose their own inputs.
    4. Write an equation that describes a relation given as a representative table or as a graph.
  • Determine whether a relation, given as a set, table, graph, or oval diagram, is a function. Sets, tables, and oval diagrams will represent finite relations, but graphs may represent infinite relations.
  • Describe functions using verbal statements and function machines.

Vocabulary

sequence
an ordered list of numbers
term
a number in a sequence
arithmetic sequence
a sequence in which the difference between two consecutive terms is constant
common difference
the difference between two consecutive terms in an arithmetic sequence
geometric sequence
a sequence in which the ratio between two consecutive terms is constant
common ratio
the ratio between two consecutive terms in a geometric sequence
plane
axis
line
vertical line
horizontal line
point
coordinate
ordered pair
quadrant
relation
a set of ordered pairs
function
a relation in which each input corresponds to exactly one output
