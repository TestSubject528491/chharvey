Preparation

Objectives

  • The student will compute the value of a numeric expression involving the four basic operations, parentheses, and exponents of whole numbers.

Essential Questions

  • What is an exponent?
  • How do I know which operations to perform first?

Vocabulary

Lesson Implementation

Warm-up

Give students the following problems to complete and turn in. Check for understanding.

  1. \(15\div 5+1\times 18\)
  2. \((20-2)\times(8+2)+10\)

Instruction

Hand students the Exponents mini-lesson. Quickly review what an exponent really is (repeated multiplication) and how to calculate it on the calculator. Have them do some example problems. This whole process should take about 15 to 20 minutes.

Exponents practice problems.

Expand the following:

  1. \(3^8\)
  2. \(n^7\)
  3. \(a^2 b^4\)

Write each as an exponent:

  1. \(2\cdot 2\cdot 2\)
  2. \(4\cdot 4\cdot 5\cdot 5\cdot 5\)
  3. \(aaabbc\)

Guided Practice

Introduce problems involving expressions where the exponent is applied to monomials only (below). After grouping symbols, they should compute the exponents, and then use the process learned from Lesson 1.1.

  1. \(5+8^2+6\)
  2. \(4+\left(9+2^2\div 2\right)\)
  3. \(3\times\left(2\times 4^3\right)\div 4\)

After practicing with monomials, introduce problems with exponents applied to binomials. Stress the importance of evaluating the parentheses before computing the exponent.

  1. \(8+\left(5+2\right)^2\)
  2. \(3^2+\left(24\div 3+3^2\right)\)
  3. \(\left[\left(10-3\right)^2\times 2\right]+7^2\)

Assessment

Formative: Check the practice problems for correctness.
