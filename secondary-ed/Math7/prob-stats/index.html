Unit 6: Probability & Statistics

Updated Dec 14, 2013.

This unit explores relations between two variables and patterns of integers and rational numbers. Students will explore different forms of relations using a variety of representations, and will identify and derive rules for functions. They will also analyze and generalize sequences of integers and rational numbers.

Learning Objectives

Relations

  • Graph individual points in the Cartesian plane.
    • given an ordered pair, plot the point in the plane
    • given a point in the plane, determine its x- and y-coordinates
    • identify Quadrants in the Cartesian plane
  • 7.12 Represent relations with tables, graphs, rules, and words.
    1. Represent and convert finite relations among a set of ordered pairs, a table, a graph, and an oval diagram. Finite relations may or may not be linear.
    2. Represent and convert infinite, linear relations among a representative table (comprising of sample values), a graph, or an equation (of the form \(y=\frac{p}{q}x+b\), or \(x=b\), where \(p\), \(q\), and \(b\) are integers).
      1. Match a relation's graph and a representative table.
      2. Construct a representative table of a relation given as an equation.
      3. Graph a relation given as an equation. Students will graph relations on a point-by-point basis, using sample points. Students will calculate the outputs of sample points given the inputs, and will also choose their own inputs.
      4. Write an equation that describes a relation given as a representative table or as a graph.
    3. Determine whether a relation, given as a set, table, graph, or oval diagram, is a function. Sets, tables, and oval diagrams will represent finite relations, but graphs may represent infinite relations.
    4. Describe functions using verbal statements and function machines.

Sequences

  • 7.2 Describe and represent arithmetic and geometric sequences, using variable expressions.
    • Investigate patterns of sequences of rational numbers. Patterns will be recursive, that is, the next term in the sequence will be determined by its predecessor. Recursive patterns will be represented by variable expressions of the following forms:
      • \(a_{n-1} + d\)
      • \(a_{n-1} \cdot r\)
    • Given an arithmetic sequence, find the common difference (\(d\)).
    • Given a geometric sequence, find the common ratio (\(r\)).
    • Create a sequence given a variable expression that describes the pattern.
