    Unit 6: Probability & Statistics

    Students will explore probability and statistics through a diverse set of media. They will investigate differences and relationships between theoretical probability and experimental probability, and calculate probabilities of simple and compound events. Students will also collect, analyze, display, and interpret data using various representations inluding histograms.

    Learning Objectives

    Simple Events

    • Compare, contrast, and apply theoretical probability and experimental probability.
    • Determine the theoretical probability of:
      • a simple event (e.g., of drawing a blue marble),
      • two alternative outcomes in a simple event (e.g., of drawing a white or blue marble), and
      • the negation of a simple event (e.g., of not drawing a red marble)
    • Determine the experimental probability of a simple event based on given data. The data may be student-collected.
    • Interpret and apply the Law of Large Numbers.

    Compound Events

    • Compound events will comprise of two simple events, which may be independent (e.g., of drawing a blue marble and flipping a coin) or dependent (e.g., of drawing a blue marble and then drawing a white marble without replacing the first one).
    • Use the Fundamental Counting Principle to determine the number of possible outcomes in a compound event.
    • Use tree diagrams to illustrate the number of possible outcomes in a compound event.
    • Determine the theoretical probability of a compound event containing two simple events.

    Histograms

    • Collect, analyze, display, and interpret a data set using histograms. For collection and display of raw data, limit the data to 20 items.
    • Determine Patterns and relationships within data sets (e.g., trends).
    • Make inferences, conjectures, and predictions based on analysis of a set of data.
    • Compare and contrast histograms with line plots, circle graphs, and stem-and-leaf plots presenting information from the same data set.

    Vocabulary

    event
    an action that occurs at random
    outcome
    the result of an event or action
    simple event
    an event for which the outcomes are disjoint (e.g., rolling a die)
    compound event
    a combination of several simple events (e.g., rolling a die and flipping a coin)
    independent events
    events for which the outcome of one does not affect the outcome of the other
    dependent events
    events for which the outcome of one does affect the outcome of the other
    probability
    the likelihood of an event producing a favorable outcome
    theoretical probability
    expected, based on a formula
    \(\frac{\text{the number of favorable outcomes possible}}{\text{the number of total outcomes possible}}\)
    experimental probability
    observable, based on actual events
    \(\frac{\text{the number of favorable occurances produced}}{\text{the number of total trials conducted}}\)
    the Law of Large Numbers
    “As the number of trials increases, the experimental probability gets closer to the theoretical probability.”
    the Fundamental Counting Principle
    “The number of possible outcomes of several events together is the product of the number of possible outcomes of each event separately.”
