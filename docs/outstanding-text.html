Outstanding Text in HTML5

Updated January 29, 2012.

Abstract

This document was written in attempt to organize and simplify common and key uses of outstanding text. When writing documents and Web sites in HTML, authors use many different semantics and styles to highlight diction, and the most common are discussed and distinguished here. Although it may appear so, this is not in any way an attempt to explain the differences between the previous and current version of HTML. It is merely a summary to use as a reference. In the descriptions of items, direct quotes (with links) of the W3C Recommendations are included, supplemented by my own interpretations. I have also supplied more examples for further clarification.

Semantic Markup

It is important to recognize that the elements in Semantic Markup serve a solely logical purpose. The elements discussed below represent parts of a document that convey meaning, rather than how they are presented. Styles and stylesheets can be changed to reformat the presentation of these elements, and stylistic aspects are discussed in another section. Readers beware, nevertheless, that this page abides by some purposeful style rules, and perhaps those unintended by the rendering browser.

Descriptions of Outstanding Semantics

The em Element

The em element represents stress emphasis of its contents. The level of emphasis that a particular piece of content has is given by its number of ancestor em elements. The placement of emphasis changes the meaning of the sentence [importance added]. The element thus forms an integral part of the content. The precise way in which emphasis is used in this way depends on the language.

Authors should use the em element to represent words or phrases that are stressed, as if the tone of voice of the speaker speaking the phrase would be affected. More importantly, the placement of stress should affect the meaning of the passage. If the emphasis were to be removed or relocated, the passage's meaning should change.

The strong Element

The strong element represents strong importance for its contents. The relative level of importance of a piece of content is given by its number of ancestor strong elements; each strong element increases the importance of its contents. Changing the importance of a piece of text with the strong element does not [emphasis added] change the meaning of the sentence.

Authors should use the strong element to represent text that has a level of importance. Important text does not necessarily affect tone of voice (although it may), but rather upsets the normal prose by causing the reader to examine the text more carefully. The placement of importance should not affect the meaning of the passage.

The mark Element

The mark element represents a run of text in one document marked or highlighted for reference purposes, due to its relevance in another context. When used in a quotation or other block of text referred to from the prose, it indicates a highlight that was not originally present but which has been added to bring the reader's attention to a part of the text that might not have been considered important by the original author when the block was originally written, but which is now under previously unexpected scrutiny. When used in the main prose of a document, it indicates a part of the document that has been highlighted due to its likely relevance to the user's current activity.

Authors should use the mark element to represent phrases that are relevant to a particular subject in a different context. In an article about black holes, for example, the author may want to mark up anything that has to do with the Law of Conservation of Information in one way, and mark up any phrases involving string theory in another way (using classes, perhaps—the reviewer could style different contexts accordingly). The mark element is ideal because these two subjects are not directly related to the article's main topic. Readers will be drawn to the corresponding subjects more efficiently.

The i Element

The i element represents a span of text in an alternate voice or mood, or otherwise offset from the normal prose, such as a taxonomic designation, a technical term, an idiomatic phrase from another language, a thought, a ship name, or some other prose whose typical typographic presentation is italicized.

Authors should use the i element to represent phrases that are conveyed in an alternate voice, mood, or language, and some proper nouns such as periodical titles or ship names, etc. As the W3C states, the <i> tag should mark up elements that are typically presented in italic text, but not necessarily. Personally, it seems this definition is a bit cyclic and somewhat depends on presentation. I like to use this element, along with italic presentation, to represent journal titles and book titles in APA citations. Contrary to the W3C specification, I would not use the <i> tag to mark up technical terms. For those, I would use the <b> tag.

The b Element

The b element represents a span of text to be stylistically offset from the normal prose without conveying any extra importance, such as key words in a document abstract, product names in a review, or other spans of text whose typical typographic presentation is boldened… Another case where the b element is appropriate is in marking up the lede (or lead) sentence or paragraph… The b element should be used as a last resort when no other element is more appropriate.

Authors should use the b element to represent text that indicates smaller proper nouns or specific terms. As the W3C states, the <b> tag should mark up elements that are typically presented in bold text, but not necessarily. Although some proper nouns are conventionally italicized such as periodical or book titles, there are some proper nouns of smaller significance, such as song and movie titles (not typically boldened) or headings in a document or table (typically boldened). I disagree with the W3C on marking up the lede sentence/paragraph with the <b> tag. For that, I would use a sub-heading, or I would request an entirely new element altogether. I think of the b element as a miscellaneous element, to be used when nothing else is suitable.
