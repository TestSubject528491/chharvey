Lists and Prose

This section describes common ways of combining lists with normal prose. The first method is used for listing whole blocks of content, for example a description after each list item. The second method is for using lists within prose.

A “List of Prose”, or a LOP, is simply a .List.O Object whose child .Items contain .P Objects.

  1. A LOP is a .List.O whose .Items consist of prose. Each .Item contains only .P Objects.

  2. Typically, each list item in a prose list will have a lede sentence. The lede sentence is treated like an actual list item in a short list, if the rest of the prose were not present. It can also be be thought of as a lede or “heading” for the text, although it does not take part in the Document Outline. The lede sentence must be an .S.Lede Object at the beginning of the .Item.

  3. Sometimes, a prose list item may have a lede that acts like a heading.

    In this case, it must be a .P.Lede Object. Again, this lede does not affect the Document Outline.

    Note: Though literal h1s are technically permissible inside lis, they usually make the Document Outline look icky, so we don't use them.

Combining p and ol Elements, we can create what is known as a “List in Prose,”, or a LIP. Three things happen with this construction:

  1. we use three Objects: a p.S, an ol.List.LIP, and another p.S, all representing inline spans of text, contained within a div.P, which represents the paragraph Object;
  2. the ol.List.LIP > li.Item Objects also appear inline, as if they were part of the paragraph itself; and
  3. the containing Paragraph Object, represented by the div.P, is still displayed as a block so that its surrounding siblings do not appear on the same line.

With these three taking place, one can utilize the semantics of the ordered list while still maintaining the style of continuous prose.

Punctuation

First and foremost, punctuation should be used to mark up text. The meaning we give to textual elements in the written language is primarily conveyed through punctuation, and if that fails, we turn to HTML. I'm not saying HTML is bad and that we shouldn't use it, but there is a line between not enough markup and too much markup.

Let me ask you this: do you find yourself trying to decide whether to wrap a question with something like <i class="question">? After all, a question does indicate a change of tone. No, we would rather simply type a question mark. Now imagine having to mark up every sentence with <sntc> tag, or even worse, every word with <word>.

Truth be told, question marks, periods, and spaces give meaning to the sentences and words we write. Likewise, we should make the best use we can of spaces, commas, apostrophes, stops (including periods, question marks, and exclamation marks) hyphens, colons, dashes, ellipses, semicolons, quotation marks, and any other punctuation characters at our disposal, as we normally would on a typewriter.

Without further ado, the number-one rule is this: if you can use punctuation to mark up text-level content, do it. Save yourself the time and effort on trying to decide on an HTML Element and just write naturally (as long as you consistently adhere to some prescribed manual of style). After that is all said and done, then it would be time to turn to the HTML Elements we know and love.

This document is not meant to be a comprehensive manual for the written English language, so it is left to the discrection of the author (and/or his employer) to decide how to use punctuation. Read further on common practices of using punction.

Emphasis

em emphasized text (stress in tone of voice)

represent words or phrases that are stressed, as if the tone of voice would be affected. More importantly, the placement of stress should affect the meaning of the passage. If the emphasis were to be removed or relocated, the passage's meaning should change.

em > em further emphasis

adds a level of emphasis

Importance

strong important text

represent text that has a level of importance. Important text does not necessarily affect tone of voice (although it may), but rather upsets the normal prose by causing the reader to examine the text more carefully. The placement of importance should not affect the meaning of the passage.

Relevance

mark relevant text

represent phrases that are relevant to a particular subject in a different context. In an article about black holes, for example, the author may want to mark up anything that has to do with the Law of Conservation of Information in one way, and mark up any phrases involving String Theory in another way (using classes, the reviewer could style different contexts accordingly). mark is ideal because these two subjects are not directly related to the article's main topic. Readers will be drawn to the corresponding subjects more efficiently.

Altered Tone of Voice

i.thought silent thought

represents an altered voice in the form of a thought

i[lang="it"] foreign language

can be used to mark up phrases in foreign languages, such as the taxonomic classification (in Latin) of an organism.
