This page includes instructional material that you might find useful class or when doing homework. Most of the links below lead to external pages.

Exponent, Radical, and Logarithmic Rules

Check out my YouTube playlist of exponent rules!

This site lists the basic rules of exponents, and includes practice problems.

For rules involving radicals, another site includes definitions and examples. This site also explains common mistakes that students make regarding these rules.

Radicals (roots) are the inverses of powers, and logarithms are the inverses of exponents. Here's a site with general logarithm rules.

Trigonometric Identities

This resource is a web site full of cool diagrams and explanations of the Trigonometric Identities. Not only does it group the formulas into categories, but it provides a proof of a few identites! So you can use this page to help remember your trig identities, and if you're extra ambitious, you can learn how and why some of them work!

Another website with proofs of more complicated Trig Idens. This site makes connections to harder concepts such as Euler's Formula, complex numbers, and polar coordinates. If you think you can take the heat, take a peek!

Calculus Rules

This small Web page contains basic differentiation rules, in addition to derivatives of exponential, logarithmic, trigonometric, and inverse trigonometric functions. Don't forget to use the chain rule!

This is a large table of integrals with every form you can possibly think of. There are no proofs included.

This website has 78 different interactive applets useful for reviewing topics in calculus. They are also great for lesson plans.

Like most students, you may have difficulty interpreting the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. What does it actually say? Check out these comprehensive and visually interactive examples, applications, and proofs of the Theorem.

What's the \(dx\) for, anyway? Find out all about differentials and what they actually mean.

Real and Complex Analysis

Ever wonder how you would graph a complex function? Well, you can't. We would need to live in a four-dimensional world to see such a graph. However, you may be able to see one or a few points at a time to get a brief picture.

Statistics

This normal distribution calculator finds the area under any normal distribution between two bounds, including infinite bounds. Use this tool in case you don't have your graphing calculator with you.
