Logical Variables and Expressions

A set is that which may own sets.

Given sets \(s\) and \(t\), the assertion that \(s\) owns \(t\) is denoted \(t\in s\). We may also say that \(t\) is an element of, or a member of, \(s\).

To avoid ambiguity, we will not use the word “contain” to represent ownership. For historical reasons, this expression can be interpreted as an indication of ownership or one of inclusion.

A proposition is a statement about sets. A proposition may or may not make an assertion about ownership.

A proposition may be true or false.

When making the assertion that a proposition \(\l p\) is true, we state \(\l p\).

Given sets \(s\) and \(t\), the statement “\(t\in s\)” is a proposition.

Given a proposition \(\l p\), the negation \(\lnot\l p\) is a proposition defined by the statement “\(\l p\) is false.”

When making the assertion that a proposition \(\l p\) is false, we state \(\lnot\l p\).

We use the proposition \(t\not\in s\) to refer to the negation \(\lnot(t\in s)\).

Given propositions \(\l p\) and \(\l q\), the disjunction \(\l p\lor\l q\) is a proposition defined by the statement “\(\l p\) or \(\l q\) (or both) is true.”

Given propositions \(\l p\) and \(\l q\), the conjunction \(\l p\land\l q\) is a proposition defined by the the statement “\(\l p\) and \(\l q\) are both true.”

Given propositions \(\l p\) and \(\l q\), the (material) implication \(\l p\implies\l q\) is a proposition defined by the disjunction \(\lnot\l p\lor\l q\).

Given an implication \(\mathsf{r}\) defined by \(\l p\implies\l q\),

  • the implication \(\l q\implies\l p\) is called the converse of \(\mathsf{r}\).
  • the implication \(\lnot\l p\implies\lnot\l q\) is called the inverse of \(\mathsf{r}\).
  • the implication \(\lnot\l q\implies\lnot\l p\) is called the contrapositive of \(\mathsf{r}\).

Given propositions \(\l p\) and \(\l q\), the (material) equivalence \(\l p\iff\l q\) is a proposition defined by the conjunction \((\l p\implies\l q)\land(\l q\implies\l p)\).

When \(\l p\lor\l q\) is true, we state, \(\l p\) or \(\l q\).

When \(\l p\land\l q\) is true, we state, \(\l p\) and \(\l q\).

There are a number of other ways to express a material implication.
When \(\l p\implies\l q\) is true, we state,
\(\l p\) is the antecedent \(\l q\) is the consequent
\(\l p\) then \(\l q\) \(\l q\) if (when) \(\l p\)
\(\l p\) implies \(\l q\) \(\l q\) is implied by \(\l p\)
\(\l p\) is sufficient for \(\l q\) \(\l q\) is necessary for \(\l p\)
\(\l p\) is stronger than \(\l q\) \(\l q\) is weaker than \(\l p\)
There are a number of other ways to express a material equivalence.
When \(\l p\iff\l q\) is true, we state,
\(\l p\) if and only if (iff) \(\l q\)
\(\l p\) implies and is implied by \(\l q\)
\(\l p\) is necessary and sufficient for \(\l q\)
\(\l p\) is materially equivalent to \(\l q\)
\(\l p\) and \(\l q\) are materially equivalent

The Axioms of Logic

Logical Statements

A logical statement is a statement that mentions propositions.

Propositions are material statements, and we can combine them to form other propositions. For example, we may take two propositions and made a new proposition from them called a conjunction. The new proposition is an assertion that the other two propositions are true. It uses them in its construction.

In contrast, a logical statement does not use other propositions; it mentions them. A logical statement asserts nothing about the truth of the propositions it mentions; it only makes an assertion about how the propositions are related. In fact, any relationship between propositions worth stating is true, i.e. a logical statement exists if and only if it is true (either accepted or proven). This is not the case for propositions: a proposition may exist if it is false.

When the context is ambiguous, we will use the term “statement” (sometimes proceeded by the term “material”) to refer to material statements, also known as propositions. To refer to logical statements we will simply use “logical statement”. In general, any complete sentence in spoken word may be referred by “assertion”.

A logical axiom is a logical statement that exists without justification.

A logical theorem is a logical statement whose existence is justified by a sequence of propositions, in an order prescribed by any previously-established logical axioms and theorems.

A logical proof is a sequence of propositions used to justify the existence of a logical theorem.

A proposition \(\l p\) logically implies a proposition \(\l q\) iff \(\l q\) is permitted to follow \(\l p\) in a logical proof according to a logical axiom or theorem. This logical implication is denoted \(\l p\therefore\l q\).

There are a number of other ways to express a logical implication.
When \(\l p\therefore\l q\), we state,
\(\l p\) is the premise \(\l q\) is the conclusion
assuming \(\l p\), conclude \(\l q\)
\(\l p\) therefore \(\l q\) \(\l q\) because \(\l p\)
\(\l p\) is followed by \(\l q\) \(\l q\) follows \(\l p\)

Propositions \(\l p\) and \(\l q\) are logically equivalent iff \(\l p\therefore\l q\) and \(\l q\therefore\l p\). This logical equivalence is denoted \(\l p\equiv\l q\).

If a logical theorem is of the form \(\l p\therefore\l q\), its proof consists of a sequence of propositions starting with \(\l p\) and ending with \(\l q\). What happens in the middle must follow the laws of the axioms and theorems already established.

If a logical theorem is of the form \(\l p\equiv\l q\), its proof must show both \(\l p\therefore\l q\) and \(\l q\therefore\l p\).

A proposition \(\l p\) is logically equivalent to the tautology \(\top\) if and only if \(\l p\) is true independent of its materials.

A proposition \(\l p\) is logically equivalent to the contradiction \(\bot\) if and only if \(\l p\) is false independent of its materials.

It might be hard to imagine a proposition that is true regardless of its contents. After all, a proposition could have at least two possible values (and we will see shortly that there are exactly two possibilities). But take for example the proposition \(\l p\) defined by \(\l q\implies\l q\) (this is called the Law of Identity). No matter what truth value \(\l q\) holds, the implication \(\l p\) must be true. Therefore we would say that \(\l p\) is a tautology, and that it could be replaced by \(\top\) in a logical proof.

Of course, informally, we could come up with many different forms of propositions, and verify the truth values of these forms using a truth table. However in order to use them in a formal system, we must start off with some axioms that allow us to make such replacements.

We are now ready to begin our journey into the axiomatic system. Let us start with an axiom so simple it almost need not be stated.

The Principle of Bivalence

  • The Law of Excluded Middle It is always the case that a proposition is true or false.

    \[\l p \lor \lnot\l p \equiv \top\]

  • The Law of Non-Contradiction It is never the case that a proposition is both true and false.

    \[\l p \land \lnot\l p \equiv \bot\]

Until this point there has always been the possibility that a proposition \(\l p\) could be both true and false, or that it could be neither. In fact, this may be the case in some abstract, many-valued branches of logic. In traditional logic however, the values true and false are both collectively exhaustive and mutually exclusive.

The Principle of Bivalence is a logical statement (in two parts) that describes not only the nature of the logical system, but also the relationship between a proposition and its negation. The first part says that one or the other must be true, and the second part says that they both cannot be true. Indeed, the disjunction \(\l p \lor \lnot\l p\) is true no matter what the value of \(\l p\), and likewise, the conjunction disjunction \(\l p \land \lnot\l p\) is false independent of \(\l p\). From this, we can explicitly prove the following obvious conjecture.

The Law of Double Negation A proposition is true if and only if its negation is false.

\[\l p \equiv \lnot\lnot\l p\]

If \(\lnot\l p\) is the assertion that \(\l p\) is false, then the assertion that \(\lnot\l p\) is false, \(\lnot\lnot\l p\), namely, the assertion that the assertion that \(\l p\) is false is false, is the assertion that \(\l p\) is true.

Rules of Inference and Proofs

A (material) axiom is a proposition that is true without justification.

Socrates is mortal.

The above example is a proposition that is clearly true. We may choose to accept this proposition without justification, in which case it would be dubbed an axiom, or we may choose to prove this statement using a sequence of other pieces of information (which in turn may be axioms), in which case the statement would be dubbed a theorem.

A (material) theorem is a proposition whose truth is justified by a sequence of previously-established theorems or axioms, in an order prescribed by a set of Rules of Inference. We call this sequence a “(material) proof”.

Socrates is mortal or immortal.

This example is another proposition (specifically, a disjunction), and we can tell by its contents (its “materials”) that it is true, so it may be an axiom or theorem, depending on the system we have set up. If it is a theorem, we would have to construct a proof, which would comprise of a sequence of other propositions that are known or have been shown to be true.

Generally though, this proposition is of the form \(\l p\lor\lnot\l p\) (a tautology). One might infer that all tautologies are true, however we have not yet the tools to verify that conjecture. In order to make such a jump, we need the following definition.

A proposition \(\l p\) logically implies a proposition \(\l p\), when and only when their implication \(\l p\implies\l q\) is a tautology.

Two propositions are logically equivalent when and only when their biconditional statement is a tautology.

Predicate Logic

The Principle of Bivalence, Predicate Version Given sets \(t\) and \(s\):

  • The Law of Excluded Middle It is always the case that \(t\) is an element of \(s\) or not an element of \(s\).

    \[(t\in s) \lor \lnot(t\in s)\]

  • The Law of Non-Contradiction It is never the case that \(t\) is both an element of \(s\) and not an element of \(s\).

    \[\lnot\bigl((t\in s) \land \lnot(t\in s)\bigr)\]

use Principle of Bivalence, Proposition Version

Given a set \(x\), the predicate \(\l P_x\) is a proposition that makes an assertion involving \(x\).

In the natural numbers, let \(\l P_n\) be the predicate \(n\) is even. Then \(\l P_3\) would be false, while \(\l P_6\) would be true.

Given a predicate \(\l P_x\), the assertion that there exists a set \(x\) that makes \(\l P_x\) true is itself a predicate, called an existential quantifier.

\[(\exists x)(\l P_x)\]

We will sometimes use the predicate \((\not\exists x)(\l P_x)\) to refer to the negation \(\lnot\bigl((\exists x)(\l P_x)\bigr)\), however we will see in D-forall that this notation—and even the predicate itself—is not necessary; the concept of negating an existential quantifier is encapsulated in another kind of predicate, called a universal quantifier.

The motivation for such an object arises from this: if there is an assertion that there exists a set that forces some predicate to be true, then the negation of that assertion is equivalent to the assertion that that predicate is automatically false for every set.

The same is true for the value of the predicate: If we negate the assertion that there exists a set that forces the predicate to be false, then we are asserting that the predicate must be true for every set.

Given a predicate \(\l P_x\), the assertion that there does not exist any set \(x\) that makes \(\l P_x\) false is itself a predicate, \((\not\exists x)(\lnot\l P_x)\), called a universal quantifier. This is the assertion that \(\l P_x\) is true for any set \(x\).

\[(\forall x)(\l P_x)\]

Given a predicate \(\l P_x\) and a set \(y\), the assertion that there exists an element \(x\) of \(y\) that makes \(\l P_x\) true is a predicate called a bound existential quantifier.

\[ \left((\exists x\in y)(\l P_x)\right) \equiv \left((\exists x)(x\in y \land \l P_x)\right) \]

let y be a set with members a, b, c, ... z. Then
\[ \left((\exists x\in y)(\l P_x)\right) \iff \left( \l P_a \lor \l P_b \lor \l P_c \lor \dots \lor \l P_z \right) \]

Given a predicate \(\l P_x\) and a set \(y\), the assertion that \(\l P_x\) is true for all elements \(x\) of \(y\) is a predicate called a bound universal quantifier.

\[ (\forall x\in y)(\l P_x) \equiv (\forall x)((x\in y) \implies \l P_x) \]

\[ \bigl((\forall x\in y)(\l P_x)\bigr) \iff \bigl((\not\exists x\in y)(\lnot\l P_x)\bigr) \]

\[ (\forall x\in y)(\l P_x)\\ (\forall x)(x\in y \implies \l P_x)\\ (\forall x)(x\not\in y \lor \l P_x)\\ (\forall x)(\lnot(x\in y \land \lnot\l P_x))\\ (\not\exists x)(x\in y \land \lnot\l P_x)\\ (\not\exists x\in y)(\lnot\l P_x)\\ \]

let y be a set with members a, b, c, ... z. Then
\[ \left((\forall x\in y)(\l P_x)\right) \iff \left( \l P_a \land \l P_b \land \l P_c \land \dots \land \l P_z \right) \]

Combined Quantifiers

Given an existential quantifier \((\exists y)(\l P_{x,y})\), the existential quantifier \(\bigl(\exists x\bigr)\bigl((\exists y)(\l P_{x,y})\bigr)\) is described by the predicate:

\[(\exists x)(\exists y)(\l P_{x,y})\]

Given a universal quantifier \((\forall y)(\l P_{x,y})\), the universal quantifier \(\bigl(\forall x\bigr)\bigl((\forall y)(\l P_{x,y})\bigr)\) is described by the predicate:

\[(\forall x)(\forall y)(\l P_{x,y})\]

existential quantification is commutative

\[ \bigl((\exists x)(\exists y)(\l P_{x,y})\bigr) \equiv \bigl((\exists y)(\exists x)(\l P_{x,y})\bigr) \]

universal quantification is commutative

\[ \bigl((\forall x)(\forall y)(\l P_{x,y})\bigr) \equiv \bigl((\forall y)(\forall x)(\l P_{x,y})\bigr) \]

an existential disjunction is logically equivalent to a disjunction of existential quantifiers

a universal disjunction is logically equivalent to a disjunction of universal quantifiers

\[ \bigl((\forall x)(\l P_x \lor \l Q_x)\bigr) \equiv \bigl((\forall a)(\l P_a) \lor (\forall b)(\l Q_b)\bigr) \]

an existential conjunction logically implies a conjunction of existential quantifiers

\[ \bigl((\exists x)(\l P_x \land \l Q_x)\bigr) \therefore \bigl((\exists a)(\l P_a) \land (\exists b)(\l Q_b)\bigr) \]

a universal conjunction is logically equivalent to a conjunction of universal quantifiers

\[ \bigl((\forall x)(\l P_x \land \l Q_x)\bigr) \equiv \bigl((\forall a)(\l P_a) \land (\forall b)(\l Q_b)\bigr) \]

Uniqueness Quantifiers

A uniqueness quantifier is an assertion that there exists a particular set, and that is the only one that exists. Essentially we are asserting that there exists a set that exhibits a certain characteristic, and all sets that exhibit that characteristic are “the same.”

define: existence in the first statement; uniqueness in the second (uniqueness doesn't depend on existence)

There exists an x such that P_x is true, and for all x, if P_x is true, then for all y, P_y is true if and only if x=y.

\[ \biggl((\exists!x)(\l P_x)\biggr) \equiv \biggl( \Bigl((\exists x)(\l P_x)\Bigr) \land \Bigl( \bigl(\forall x\bigr) \bigl(\l P_x \implies (\forall y)(\l P_y \iff x=y)\bigr) \Bigr) \Biggr) \]

prove: existence in the first statement; uniqueness in the second (uniqueness doesn't depend on existence)

There exists an x such that P_x is true, and for any two x and y, if P_x and P_y are true, then x=y.

\[ \bigl((\exists!x)(\l P_x)\bigr) \equiv \bigl( (\exists x)(\l P_x) \land (\forall x)(\forall y)\left(\l P_x \land \l P_y \implies x=y\right) \bigr) \]

prove: existence and uniqueness in one statement

There exists an x such that (P_x is true and for all y, P_y is true if and only if x=y).

\[ \bigl((\exists!x)(\l P_x)\bigr) \equiv \Bigl( \bigl(\exists x\bigr) \bigl(\l P_x \land (\forall y)(\l P_y \iff x=y)\bigr) \Bigr) \]

Equality and Inclusion

The Intensionality Definition A set is equal to another set iff those sets own each other's elements.

We use the predicate \(x=y\) to describe the assertion that a set \(x\) is equal to a set \(y\). We define this predicate as:

\[ \left(x=y\right) \equiv \left((\forall t)(t\in x \iff t\in y)\right) \]

We use the predicate \(x\not= y\) to refer to the negation \(\lnot(x=y)\).

The Axiom of Extensionality Equal elements are members of the same sets.

\[ (\forall x)(\forall y) \left( \expand{x=y}{ (\forall t)(t\in x\iff t\in y) } \iff \left((\forall z)(x\in z \iff y\in z)\right) \right) \]

Intensionality states that sets that are equal have exactly the same members. We take this to be the definition of equality: equal sets are “identical.” One very important interpretation of this is that a set is determined uniquely its elements, so if sets with exactly the same elements have different definitions, names, or rearrangements, those sets are all one in the same. This may seem obvious, but a more fascinating fact about equal sets follows.

By The Axiom of Extensionality, equal sets are members of the same containers. This means that all sets are definite and distinguishable: given an element, one should be able to decide whether or not that element would belong to any particular set; and that any two distinct elements of the same set are unequal. This will come in handy later on, when we start repeating elements within a set. By this axiom, a set cannot own the same element more than once.

A set \(x\) is a subset of a set \(y\) iff \(y\) owns all the elements of \(x\).

We use the predicate \(x\subseteq y\) to describe the assertion that \(x\) is a subset of \(y\). We define this predicate as:

\[ \left(x\subseteq y\right) \equiv \expand{(\forall t\in x)(t\in y)}{(\forall t)(t\in x \implies t\in y)} \]

We also say \(x\) is included in \(y\), or \(y\) includes \(x\).

A set \(x\) is a proper subset of a set \(y\) iff \(x\subseteq y\) and \(x\not= y\).

We use the predicate \(x\subset y\) to describe the assertion that \(x\) is a proper subset of \(y\). We define this predicate as:

\[ \bigl(x\subset y\bigr) \equiv \bigl( (x\subseteq y) \land (x\not=y) \bigr) \]

We also say \(x\) is strictly included in \(y\), or \(y\) strictly includes \(x\).

Remember the distinction between \(y\) owns \(x\) and \(y\) includes \(x\).

In the former expression, we are trying to determine if \(x\) is an element of \(y\). To do so, we look at the set \(x\) as a single object, and then check to see if this object is among the members of the set \(y\). If so, \(x\in y\).

In the latter expression, we are trying to determine if \(x\) is a subset of \(y\). To do this, we must open up the set \(x\), examine its various elements, and then check to see if the set \(y\) owns every one of those elements. If so, \(x\subseteq y\).

In each case, we will avoid the term “contain” because of its ambiguous meaning in different sources. (A set could “contain” its elements or it could “contain” its subsets.)

Properties of Equality and Inclusion

Two sets are equal if and only if they are subsets of each other.

Ergo, when \(x=y\), instead of saying, \(x\) is equal to \(y\), we may just as well say, \(x\) and \(y\) are equal.
